Every town here clears the one non-negotiable: California's Lanterman entitlement covers all of them, so Maddy's services are guaranteed statewide — the difference is only which regional center holds her file, and a move between them is a transfer, not a restart.

So the gate isn't the story on this page. The new axis is: getting to Berkeley without a car. That single question reshuffles the whole board — because the towns that commute well to Berkeley sit on the Capitol Corridor rail line or a BART-bus, and the foothills don't. The finalist you already love is the outlier here.

Grass Valley wins the life. On rails to Berkeley, it doesn't run at all. The two things you want may live in different towns.

Read   Each town, up close

Martinez

▸ BEST COMMUTE
Contra Costa · Regional Center of the East Bay · ~$780K · wildfire low–moderate

The train pick. Capitol Corridor drops you in Berkeley in about 35 minutes, roughly fourteen times a day — the closest thing to a one-seat rail commute on the whole list. Historic waterfront downtown, a marina, John Muir's home, and an enormous East Bay Regional Park network at the doorstep (Briones, Carquinez Strait) for Cooper. The honest gaps: no true co-op, so you'd lean on Bay Area grocers ~20 minutes out, and the water here is Delta and bay — for trout you're driving to the Sierra.

Fairfield / Suisun City

▸ BEST VALUE
Solano · North Bay Regional Center · Suisun ~$525K / Fairfield ~$610–695K · wildfire low–moderate

The runway play — the only new town in your Grass Valley price range, with Suisun City around $525K and a direct Capitol Corridor stop that reaches Berkeley in about 55 minutes. Suisun City's waterfront district is walkable and genuinely charming; Rockville Hills Regional Park covers dog trails. The trade: it's the weakest of the five on the clean-eating co-op and the walk-everywhere vibe — Fairfield proper is car-oriented, and the LGBTQ presence is suburban rather than a scene.

Benicia

▸ MOST CHARM
Solano · North Bay Regional Center · ~$790–800K · wildfire low

The charm pick — a compact former state capital on the Carquinez Strait with one of the most walkable Main Streets in the North Bay, an arts district in the old Arsenal, waterfront everywhere, and Benicia State Recreation Area shoreline for the dog. Low wildfire risk. The catch is the commute's frequency, not its length: the express bus to BART runs only about every two hours, so life bends around the timetable.

Davis

▸ BEST LIFE + RAIL
Yolo · Alta California — same RC as A1 · ~$780–830K · wildfire low, flood moderate

The best lifestyle match of the four new towns, and the smoothest for Maddy: Davis sits in the same Alta California Regional Center as Grass Valley, so her file wouldn't even change hands. It has a true food co-op, Putah Creek's tailwater trout minutes away (your standout water), and it's one of the most bike- and dog-friendly, LGBTQ-affirming towns in the country. The cost of all that: ~$800K and tight inventory, plus hot Central Valley summers and winter tule fog.

Call   What the axis is really asking

If reaching Berkeley without a car is a hard requirement, the board tilts to the Capitol Corridor: Martinez for the shortest ride, Fairfield/Suisun for rail at foothill prices, Davis for the best blend of that commute and the life you actually want. Benicia works if you can live with a two-hour bus headway. Grass Valley keeps winning the life and loses this axis outright.

The real question underneath it: who needs Berkeley, and how often? A once-a-week trip and a five-day commute point at completely different towns — and at whether the foothill finalist survives at all.

Five towns, one gate cleared by all of them, one new axis that splits them clean down the middle. Now we know what Berkeley costs — in minutes, and in trade-offs.
